Combat Robot - Texas Robo Rumble

Final bot ready for competition

3lb Beetle-Weight Combat Robot

Designed and manufactured in 4 weeks to compete against 15 teams with a strict $300 budget. Featuring a UHMW frame, CNC-machined aluminum beater bar, and custom silicone foam wheels for maximum impact resistance and maneuverability.

Project Summary

This project engineered a 3lb beetle-weight combat robot to compete in the Texas Robo Rumble tournament. Facing predominantly vertical spinners and beater bars, we developed a resilient design featuring a UHMW frame milled on a Tormach 1100 CNC, silicone foam wheels cast in 3D-printed molds, and an aluminum beater bar with interchangeable steel screw teeth. Through four design iterations and topology optimization based on FEA, we achieved perfect weight distribution at 2.98 lbs. The robot placed 2nd out of 15 teams, demonstrating exceptional durability against opponent weapons.

Battle-Ready Engineering

Strategic Design Approach

Facing vertical spinners within strict budget and weight constraints, we prioritized impact resistance and weight distribution. UHMW polyethylene was selected for its exceptional energy absorption properties (yield modulus), low density, and cost-effectiveness. For mobility, we developed silicone foam wheels cast in 3D-printed molds, optimizing for weight savings over maximum traction since pushing power was secondary to maneuverability in the arena. This material strategy saved 40% weight versus solid rubber alternatives while providing adequate traction.

Weapon System Engineering

The aluminum beater bar was engineered for optimal mass distribution - heavy enough to transfer kinetic energy but light enough to achieve rapid spin-up times. Starting with manual mill and dropsaw roughing, we finished the component using CNC machining for precision balancing. Threaded mounts allowed quick replacement of hardened steel screw teeth between matches, maintaining offensive capability throughout the tournament. Dynamic balancing ensured minimal vibration at operational RPMs.

Beater bar design and steel screw teeth detail

Frame Development & Fabrication

Laser-cut prototypes tested various side panel configurations, with topology optimization via finite element analysis guiding the final geometry to withstand cantilever forces. The UHMW frame was precision-milled on a Tormach 1100 CNC, creating interlocking joints that distributed impact energy across the structure. Steel side panels were added as sacrificial elements to protect critical electronics from direct hits.

Tournament Performance

In the Texas Robo Rumble, our robot demonstrated exceptional resilience against vertical spinners that destroyed other competitors' bots. Post-tournament inspection revealed only minor bite marks on the UHMW frame from opponent weapons, with all critical systems remaining operational through the semifinals. Our strategic design choices proved effective: the UHMW frame absorbed impacts without cracking, modular weapon teeth enabled rapid field repairs, and optimal weight distribution prevented flipping during high-G maneuvers.

Competition Result: Placed 2nd out of 15 teams at Texas Robo Rumble, with the robot surviving direct hits from steel weapons that eliminated other competitors.

Competition highlights showing resilience against opponent weapons

Key Takeaways & Skills Utilized

This project demonstrated that material selection and weight distribution matter more than absolute armor thickness in combat robotics. We learned that UHMW's impact absorption outperformed polycarbonate at similar weights despite costing 60% less, and that center-of-mass positioning was critical for resisting flipping. Budget management became an art form, with strategic splurges on steel teeth balanced by cost-saving 3D-printed wheel molds.

Engineering Insight: The UHMW frame's performance redefined our approach to impact resistance - energy absorption through material deformation proved more effective than rigid protection against vertical spinners.
