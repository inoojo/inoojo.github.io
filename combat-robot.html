Combat Robot - Texas Robo Rumble

Final bot ready for competition

3lb Beetle-Weight Combat Robot

Designed and manufactured in 4 weeks to compete against 15 other teams with a strict $300 budget. Featuring a UHMW frame plated with stainless steel, CNC-machined aluminum beater bar, and a differential steering system for precise control and maneuverability.

Project Summary

This project focused on developing a compact, highly robust robotic system for a competitive environment under tight constraints. Over a four-week period, we engineered a 3lb beetle-weight combat robot through multiple design iterations, integrating CNC-machined components, custom-molded parts, and FEA-driven optimization. The result was a durable and agile system that placed 2nd out of 16 teams, showcasing its effectiveness in real-world testing and combat performance.

Battle-Ready Engineering

Strategic Design Approach

From the beginning, we recognized that material selection and frame construction would be the most critical factors in performance. We prioritized impact resistance and weight distribution, ultimately selecting UHMW polyethylene for the frame due to its excellent energy absorption (high yield strain), low density, and cost-effectiveness. For mobility, we initially developed custom silicone wheels cast in 3D-printed molds, but later replaced them due to weight constraints and the secondary role of traction in our competition strategy. This shift reduced wheel weight by 65%, while still providing sufficient grip for precise maneuvering.

To improve maintainability and field repairability, we adopted a modular frame architecture using machined aluminum angle brackets at the corners. Unlike a mono-part frame, this approach allowed for quick part replacement in the event of damageâ€”crucial in a high-impact environment.

Weapon System Engineering

The aluminum beater bar was engineered for optimal mass distribution - heavy enough to transfer kinetic energy but light enough to achieve rapid spin-up times. Starting with manual mill and dropsaw roughing, we finished the component using CNC machining. Threaded mounts allowed quick replacement of hardened steel screw teeth between matches, maintaining offensive capability throughout the tournament. Dynamic balancing using the adjustable teeth allowed for minimal vibration at operational RPMs.

Beater bar closeup and weapon test

Frame Development & Fabrication

Laser-cut prototypes were used to test various side panel configurations, with finite element analysis informing our final frame geometry to withstand cantilevered loading. The UHMW chassis was CNC-milled on a Tormach 1100, and assembled using custom-machined aluminum corner brackets to ensure structural rigidity while allowing for quick disassembly and repair. Stainless steel side and rear panels were added to reinforce the frame and provide additional protection to critical components, significantly increasing the robotâ€™s durability during direct impacts.

Tournament Performance

During the competition, our robot demonstrated outstanding durability under heavy impacts. Post-match inspections revealed only minor bite marks on the UHMW frame, with all core systems remaining fully operational through to the semifinals. The modular weapon teeth and frame design enabled quick field repairs between matches, helping us stay competitive under tight time constraints. Ultimately, we placed 2nd overall, with the final match lost due to a torn weapon belt that disabled our primary attack mechanism.

Competition Result: Placed 2nd out of 16 teams at Texas Robo Rumble, with the robot surviving many direct hits from steel weapons that eliminated other competitors.

Key Takeaways & Skills Utilized

Through this project, we learned that careful material selection is crucialâ€”UHMW provided the best balance between density, cost, and impact absorption for combat robotics. We also discovered that weight distribution and center of mass placement are essential for stability and resisting flips. Managing a strict budget taught us how to prioritize spending strategically, investing in critical components like steel weapon teeth while saving weight and cost in other areas. Looking ahead, we recognize the need to select a stronger material for the weapon belt to improve durability and prevent failures like the one experienced in the finals.

Engineering Insight: The UHMW frame's performance redefined our approach to impact resistance - energy absorption through material deformation proved more effective than rigid protection against impact weapons.
